About this tool
Built for Somalia MSMEs. Data from OCHA, ACLED, FSNAU, World Bank Somalia.
Context: why disaster preparedness matters for Somalia MSMEs
Climate hazards

Somalia experiences recurring drought-flood cycles (La Niña/El Niño driven). The 2022–2023 drought was the worst in 40 years, destroying crops and livestock. Coastal flooding affects fishing communities seasonally.

Conflict and access

Al-Shabaab controls or contests significant territory across south-central Somalia, disrupting supply chains, restricting market access, and creating unpredictable operating environments for MSMEs.

Business vulnerability

Most Somali MSMEs operate with thin cash reserves, limited insurance access, informal record-keeping, and heavy reliance on a small number of suppliers — making them highly vulnerable to shocks.

Overview

This dashboard calculates composite disaster risk scores for Somali MSMEs across four sectors. Scores reflect exposure to climate shocks, conflict and access disruption, business vulnerability, and preparedness gaps — weighted equally by default, then adjusted by sector-specific sensitivity multipliers.

Scoring formula

Equal-Weight Score = (Climate Risk + Conflict & Access + Business Vulnerability + Preparedness Gap) ÷ 4
Sector-Adjusted Score = Equal-Weight Score × Sector Multiplier
Final Score = MIN(Sector-Adjusted Score, 100)

Each input dimension is scored 0–100. The final sector-adjusted score is capped at 100.

Risk dimensions

Climate Risk (0–100): Drought frequency and severity (based on SPI and NDVI anomaly data), flood exposure, cyclone exposure for coastal zones, and rainfall variability. Primary source: FSNAU agro-climatic monitoring; CHIRPS rainfall data.

Conflict and Access Risk (0–100): Number of security incidents within 50km radius (ACLED), presence of armed group territorial control, checkpoint density on key trade routes, and humanitarian access constraints. Source: ACLED, OCHA Access Monitoring.

Business Vulnerability (0–100): Composite of supplier concentration (single-source dependency), cash reserve adequacy, water and energy dependency, and lack of financial buffers. Source: World Bank Somalia MSME survey, UNDP private sector assessments.

Preparedness Gap (0–100): Inverse of preparedness: early warning system coverage, business continuity planning, record backup, and access to insurance or credit recovery mechanisms. Source: FSNAU, UNDP Somalia, ILO Safer Work assessments.

Sector sensitivity multipliers

SectorMultiplierRationale

Livestock×1.3Mobility-dependent; high conflict exposure on pastoral routes
Fisheries×1.4Coastal flood/cyclone risk + piracy + cold-chain dependency
Textiles×1.1Lower climate exposure; urban supply chains, conflict risk remains

Self-assessment scoring

Each self-assessment question maps to a risk dimension. Answers are scored 1 (most vulnerable) to 4 (most resilient), then rescaled to 0–100: score = (4 - answer) / 3 × 100. This is blended with regional baseline scores (60% self-assessment, 40% regional baseline) for the final output.

Data sources
📊 FSNAU — Food Security and Nutrition Analysis Unit. Agro-climatic monitoring, IPC phase data, livelihood zone analysis. fsnau.org
⚔ ACLED — Armed Conflict Location and Event Data. Conflict incidents, displacement, access restrictions. acleddata.com
🌍 UN OCHA Somalia — Humanitarian access monitoring, situation reports, corridor assessments. unocha.org/somalia
🌦 CHIRPS — Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station data (UC Santa Barbara). Rainfall anomaly baseline.
🏦 World Bank Somalia — MSME survey data, financial inclusion, private sector assessments.
🤝 UNDP Somalia — Private sector resilience assessments, disaster preparedness frameworks.
📋 ILO Safer Work — Business continuity and occupational resilience data for informal sector.
Limitations

Regional scores are based on area-level data and may not reflect hyperlocal conditions. Conflict indicators can change rapidly. Self-assessment scores depend on self-reported answers.

This tool is intended to support decision-making, not replace on-the-ground expert assessment. For high-stakes decisions, consult OCHA, FSNAU, or a local humanitarian partner.
